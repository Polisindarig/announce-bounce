## 1. Introduction and Scope

This thesis sits at the intersection of four research streams: (i) the foundational finance literature on *event-driven trading* and information efficiency, (ii) *financial sentiment analysis* using lexicons and transformer-based language models, (iii) *cryptocurrency market microstructure* and its documented inefficiencies, and (iv) the small but growing body of work on *exchange announcement effects* in crypto. We review each in turn, then identify the research gap that motivates the present work.

The scope is deliberately narrow: we focus on **scheduled and unscheduled announcements published by centralized exchanges**, the *intraday* (sub-hour) price response, and *deployable* — not merely descriptive — trading strategies. We exclude on-chain analytics, derivatives-market microstructure beyond execution costs, and pure technical-indicator-based bots, except where they serve as baselines.

## 2. Event Studies and Information Efficiency

The classical foundation is the **Efficient Market Hypothesis** (Fama, 1970), which posits that in a *semi-strong-form efficient* market, new public information is impounded into prices essentially instantly. Empirically, departures from this idealization are studied via **event-study methodology** (Brown & Warner, 1985; MacKinlay, 1997), which compares actual returns around an event window to a counterfactual "normal" return generated by a market or factor model. The standard test statistic is the *cumulative abnormal return* (CAR):

$$\text{CAR}_i(t_1, t_2) = \sum_{t=t_1}^{t_2} \left(R_{i,t} - \mathbb{E}[R_{i,t}\mid \text{no event}]\right)$$

Two well-known anomalies — the **post-earnings-announcement drift** (Bernard & Thomas, 1989) and the **media-coverage effect** (Tetlock, 2007) — show that markets often *under-react* to qualitative information for hours to weeks, generating exploitable signals. Tetlock (2007) in particular demonstrated, using Wall Street Journal sentiment, that pessimistic media predicts downward price pressure on the Dow Jones for short horizons. This is the conceptual ancestor of every sentiment-based trading paper that followed.

For event-study methodology applied specifically to cryptocurrencies, **Zhou (2025)**, in *Emerging Markets Finance and Trade* (Vol. 61, Issue 4, pp. 989–1009), examines six major cryptocurrencies (BTC, ETH, BNB, XRP, DOGE, TRX) over Dec 2017 – Oct 2023 and demonstrates that effect magnitude and persistence depend strongly on event *type*: cybersecurity and block-reward events have minimal and short-lived effects; political conflict events drive significant short-term volatility; public-health emergencies (e.g., COVID-19) produce significant and *lasting* negative impacts; and crypto-recognition / support events produce significant *sustained* positive returns. This category-heterogeneity finding directly motivates our category-conditional strategy design.

Complementary work by **Caporin, Costola, Iseppi & Lautizi (2024)** — "Not all words are equal: Sentiment and jumps in the cryptocurrency market" (*Journal of International Financial Markets, Institutions and Money*) — shows that sentiment dimensions tied to *market fundamentals* are more strongly associated with price *jumps* than emotion-only sentiment, a key motivation for our two-stage (category → within-category sentiment) classifier.

## 3. Financial Sentiment Analysis

### 3.1 Lexicon-based methods

The first generation of financial sentiment work used hand-curated lexicons. **Loughran and McDonald (2011)** showed that the standard Harvard-IV psychosocial dictionary misclassifies finance-specific terms (e.g., "liability," "tax") and built a finance-tuned lexicon that remains a baseline benchmark. Their *Negative*, *Positive*, *Uncertainty*, and *Litigious* word lists are still used in 10-K filings analysis today.

### 3.2 Machine-learning era

Antweiler and Frank (2004) trained a Naïve Bayes classifier on stock message boards and found that posting *volume* predicts volatility more reliably than posting *sentiment* predicts returns — an important methodological warning we revisit when we design our features. Das and Chen (2007) extended this to multi-classifier ensembles.

### 3.3 Transformer era

**FinBERT** (Araci, 2019) further-pretrained BERT on a financial corpus and fine-tuned on the *Financial PhraseBank*, reaching ~89% accuracy and becoming the de facto sentiment baseline in quantitative finance. Yang, Uy, and Huang (2020) released a larger variant trained on analyst reports and earnings calls. For cryptocurrency text specifically, **CryptoBERT** (ElKulako & Pintus, 2022) was pretrained on millions of crypto-related social posts and out-performs FinBERT on crypto slang and ticker-heavy headlines.

Most recently, **LLM-based zero-shot classification** has become competitive: BloombergGPT (Wu et al., 2023) shows in-domain pretraining still matters, but Lopez-Lira and Tang (2023) demonstrate that even general-purpose GPT-4 can predict next-day stock returns from news headlines at statistically significant levels. The trade-off — flexibility vs. reproducibility, cost, and audit-ability — is central to our methodology choice (see `docs/02-methodology.md`).

### 3.4 Hybrid models for crypto

A recent and directly relevant model is **FinBERT-BiLSTM** (Hossain, Lamia, Rahman, & Khan, 2024, arXiv:2411.12748; published in *Applied Intelligence*, 2026), which feeds FinBERT sentiment scores into a Bidirectional LSTM alongside OHLCV features to forecast BTC/ETH prices. Reported MAPE is 2.03% / 2.52% intra-day and 2.20% / 2.77% one-day-ahead (BTC/ETH), outperforming pure-sentiment, pure-technical, and Transformer (Informer, TFT, TimeGPT) baselines — validating the *combination* of NLP features with market state.

## 4. Cryptocurrency Market Microstructure

Three stylized facts make crypto a fertile ground for event-driven strategies and justify why we expect findings that differ from equity markets:

1. **Documented inefficiencies.** Urquhart (2016) was the first to formally show that Bitcoin returns fail the Hurst-exponent and variance-ratio tests for weak-form efficiency. Wei (2018) extended this to altcoins, finding strong inefficiency in lower-liquidity coins — exactly the coins most affected by Binance listings.
2. **Continuous, retail-dominated trading.** Crypto markets trade 24/7 with high retail participation, no consolidated tape, and significant exchange fragmentation. Makarov and Schoar (2020, *Journal of Financial Economics*) document large persistent cross-exchange price deviations.
3. **High and time-varying volatility.** Katsiampa (2017) and many follow-ups find GARCH-family models necessary even at daily frequency; intraday volatility clustering is even more pronounced. This matters for our TP/SL calibration: a 2% target on BTC is a different bet than a 2% target on a new low-cap listing.

## 5. Exchange Announcement Effects in Crypto (Most Directly Relevant)

This is the literature our work most directly extends.

### 5.1 The "exchange listing effect"

**Ante (2019)**, in *Blockchain Research Lab Working Paper Series, No. 3* ("Market Reaction to Exchange Listings of Cryptocurrencies"), analyzed **327 listings of 180 cryptocurrencies across 22 exchanges** and found an average abnormal return of **+5.7% on listing day** and **+9.2% over a 7-day window (−3 to +3)**. Effects differ dramatically across exchanges: **Binance showed +14.7%**, **Bitfinex +25.5%**, **Bittrex +23.5%** on listing day. Crucially, abnormal returns of **+3.2% in the window (−3, −2) before the official announcement** indicate informed trading / information leakage. Additional tests show that *higher market cap combined with lower trading volume* predicts larger listing-day abnormal returns.

A 2023 industry analysis widely cited as the **"Binance effect"** (Sigalos, *CoinDesk*, January 6, 2023) reported newly listed Binance tokens spike **+41% one day after listing**, **+24% on day three**, and **+73% on average over the first 30 days**.

**However, longer-horizon analysis tells the opposite story.** A 7-year industry study of 500+ Binance listings (*Empirica*, 2024) found tokens **lost an average of 6.34% in the first week** post-listing, only **5.5% of tokens were positive after 6 months**, and on average underperformed ETH by **−39.46% over 6 months**. This implies the listing effect is an *intraday-to-multiday* phenomenon — exactly the horizon our bot targets — and that *holding beyond the event window destroys alpha*. This finding directly informs our exit logic: time-stop is mandatory.

Comparative work presented at **AFT 2025** (Advances in Financial Technologies, Dagstuhl proceedings) finds that more regulated exchanges (e.g., Coinbase) generate higher listing-day returns than less regulated ones (e.g., Binance), attributing the difference to a "stamp of approval" / due-diligence signal, and notes that Binance is more likely to list tokens with weaker fundamentals (sometimes from its own VC arm). This motivates our hypothesis that *category and listing context* matter, not just the existence of an announcement.

### 5.2 Beyond listings

Other announcement types are far less studied. **"The Impact of Announcements on Cryptocurrency Prices"** (*Revista Economica*, Vol. 76, Issue 4, pp. 69–94, 2024) provides category-level evidence that volatile assets like Dogecoin experience pronounced and longer-lasting price changes from positive announcements, while BTC/ETH show briefer responses; negative regulatory actions produce *prolonged* detrimental effects; and investor sentiment amplifies both positive and negative event effects. Combined with Zhou (2025) (§2), the cross-event-type evidence is:
- **Listings / exchange-recognition / support events** → significant short-term positive abnormal returns; mean-reversion thereafter.
- **Cybersecurity events (hacks, exploits)** → significant short-term negative returns; relatively fast mean-reversion.
- **Regulatory enforcement (e.g., SEC actions)** — see also Aharon, Demir & Goodell (arXiv:2412.02452, 2024) — produce **−12% over the week post-announcement, persisting up to a month**.
- **Block-reward adjustments / halvings** → minimal effect on returns (volatility effect documented separately).
- **Public-health emergencies** → large, persistent negative returns.

This heterogeneity is the empirical evidence that **a single "announcement effect" parameter is insufficient** — a category-conditional model is required.

### 5.3 Pre-announcement leakage

Ante's (2019) finding of +3.2% abnormal return *before* the announcement is methodologically critical for us. It implies the "official" timestamp on a Binance announcement is **not** the actual information-arrival timestamp. Our methodology must (i) define a strict event window, (ii) treat pre-announcement drift as a robustness check rather than a tradable signal (we cannot trade what we cannot observe ex ante), and (iii) use an out-of-sample window long enough that the bot's *observed* fill prices reflect any post-announcement slippage realistically.

## 6. Sentiment-Driven Crypto Strategies

Mai, Shan, Bai, Wang, and Chiang (2018) showed that Twitter and forum sentiment Granger-cause Bitcoin price movements at daily frequency. Phillips and Gorse (2017) used hidden Markov models on online sentiment to detect crypto bubbles. Kraaijeveld and De Smedt (2020) used VADER on 1.6 million tweets and found predictive power for *some* altcoins but not BTC/ETH — suggesting **noisy signals work for low-cap, high-attention assets**, which again aligns with newly-listed Binance tokens.

Chen, Liu, and Zhao (2020) constructed a sentiment-based trading strategy on Reddit's r/CryptoCurrency and reported Sharpe ratios > 1 at daily frequency — but did not deploy strict out-of-sample testing, which is a methodological weakness we explicitly address (see §9 on backtesting pitfalls).

The recurring critique of this sub-literature is that retail-social-media sentiment is **endogenous to price**: prices rise, people tweet, sentiment indicators rise, and the bot "predicts" a movement that has already happened. Using **the exchange's own scheduled announcements** as the information source partially solves this — the source is exogenous to the market reaction it triggers.

## 7. Algorithmic Trading and Position Sizing

For the strategy layer, the canonical position-sizing rule is the **Kelly criterion** (Kelly, 1956), with practical refinements by Thorp (2006). Kelly maximizes expected *log-wealth* but is empirically too aggressive; "fractional Kelly" (e.g., 25%–50% of full Kelly) is standard in quantitative practice. We adopt fractional Kelly conditioned on **category-specific win-rate and average win/loss estimated in-sample**, with a hard per-trade cap.

For the strategy *structure*, we draw on Chan (2013) for event-driven framework design and López de Prado (2018, *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*) for backtest hygiene — particularly his treatment of *triple-barrier labeling* (which essentially formalizes "first-of TP / SL / time-stop hits" — exactly our exit logic).

Reinforcement-learning approaches to crypto trading (Jiang & Liang, 2017; Liu et al., 2020, *FinRL*) are an alternative paradigm. We do **not** adopt RL here, for three reasons: (1) data efficiency — RL needs orders of magnitude more episodes than we have announcement events; (2) interpretability — a thesis defends better with explicit decision rules; (3) overfitting risk — RL agents are notorious for in-sample memorization in finance.

## 8. Backtesting Pitfalls

Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado, and Zhu (2014), in *The Probability of Backtest Overfitting*, formalize how easily backtest results inflate under repeated trial. The three most relevant pitfalls for our project are:

1. **Look-ahead bias.** Sentiment models trained on the *full* corpus then applied "in-sample" leak future information. Mitigation: all NLP models trained only on first-N-month text; sentiment-score function frozen before touching the OOS window.
2. **Survivorship bias.** A token that was delisted from Binance after our analysis but still traded during it must remain in the dataset; otherwise we systematically over-estimate listing returns. We will use a *point-in-time* universe.
3. **Transaction-cost neglect.** Binance taker fees (~0.10% pre-discounts), spread on newly listed tokens (often 50–200 bps in the first minutes), and slippage at retail order sizes are non-trivial. We model each explicitly.

Walk-forward analysis (Pardo, 2008) — re-fitting parameters in rolling windows and testing on the next window — is preferred to a single in-sample/out-of-sample split when sample size permits. Given only 24 months of in-sample data, we use a single split plus *parameter stability tests* across rolling sub-windows as a robustness check.

## 9. Research Gap and Our Contribution

Synthesizing the above, the published literature has independently established:
- That Binance listings produce large positive abnormal returns at daily frequency.
- That announcement *type* matters — different categories produce different responses.
- That transformer-based sentiment classifiers (FinBERT, CryptoBERT) work on financial text.
- That information leaks before "official" announcement times.
- That backtests overfit easily without strict OOS protocols.

What has **not** been done in any single peer-reviewed paper we could locate is the integration of all five into a deployable, intraday, multi-category event-driven strategy with out-of-sample walk-forward validation. Specifically:

| Aspect | Existing literature | This thesis |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Twitter, Reddit, news headlines | Binance's own official announcement feed (low-noise) |
| **Categories** | Listings (mostly), occasional regulatory | 8+ categories: listing, delisting, futures-listing, partnership/integration, security incident, regulatory, fork, staking/airdrop |
| **Horizon** | Daily abnormal returns | 1 min, 5 min, 15 min, 1 h, 4 h, 24 h |
| **Sentiment integration** | Either sentiment-only or category-only | Two-stage: category classifier → within-category sentiment scorer |
| **Position sizing** | Equal-weight or none | Category-conditional fractional Kelly with volatility scaling |
| **Risk management** | Often absent | Per-category TP/SL calibrated on in-sample distribution percentiles |
| **Validation** | Often full-sample or weak OOS | Strict 24 m / 6 m split, frozen-model OOS, parameter-stability checks |

This combination is, to our knowledge, novel and constitutes the contribution of the thesis.
